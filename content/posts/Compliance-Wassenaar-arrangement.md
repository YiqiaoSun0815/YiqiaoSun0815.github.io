During my work, I come across transactions involving **dual-use goods**.  
These are goods that can be used for civilization purposes, but may also have potential military applications. In such situations, understanding international export control frameworks becomes essential.So I do some searching and found the 
**Wassenaar Arrangement**.

## What is the Wassenaar Arrangement

The Wassenaar Arrangement is a multilateral export control regime established in 1996. Its purpose is to promote transparency and responsibility in the transfer of:

- conventional arms  
- dual-use goods and technologies  

Dual-use items include products such as high-precision machine tools, certain metals, advanced electronics, and other technologies that can potentially be used in military production.

Participating states exchange information and maintain control lists to prevent these goods from contributing to regional instability or military misuse.

## A case I encountered at work

I've found a special-mentioned goods in one of my client's FS, the client was involved in the sale of a product that could be classified as **dual-use equipment**.

To dig in,the steps I took were relatively straightforward:

1. **Checked the client's export license** to confirm that the product could legally be exported.

2. **Reviewed the full supply chain** to identify the final end user.  
   After investigation, the end user was confirmed not to be located in Russia, Iran, or any sanctioned jurisdiction.

3. **Verified whether the destination country permits the import** of this type of product.

4. **Confirmed the import license of the end user**, ensuring the buyer had the legal authorization to receive the goods.

These steps helped establish further understanding of the Wassenaar Arrangement.

## Takeaway

In today’s environment, geopolitical tensions and conflicts are becoming increasingly complex. For bankers working in international trade and finance, sanctions and export controls are no longer niche topics.

For me personally, working in Switzerland — a major hub for international trade and commodity flows — it has become clear that continuous learning in sanctions, export control regimes, and compliance frameworks is essential. Understanding these rules is not only about regulatory compliance, but also about protecting the bank from legal and reputational risks.
